Does polluters pay the price of their emissions ?

By Emmanuelle Picaud

Every day, thousands of companies across Europe buy and sell the right to pollute on the the European Union Emissions Trading System (EU ETS).

The idea is simple: make pollution expensive enough that cutting emissions becomes the cheaper option. Today, this market is the world’s leading financial platform of its kind, though others are emerging, such as in China.

My data analysis asks a straightforward question: has the market delivered on that promise of forcing polluters to complain and reduce their emissions ?

The data analysis shows the 27 EU countries under EU ETS regulation are not equal regarding the right to pollute they receive. German installations are far away the main receivers. This explains because Germany's position largely reflects the size of its economy and its concentration of ETS-covered activities, including power generation and energy-intensive industries such as steel, chemicals, cement and refining. It also does have the highest number of factories.

The discrepancies are not only at the country-scale : among industrials under the EU ETS, all are not progressing at the same rythm. This explain due to several factors. For combustion of fuels, which mainly represents electricity generation, the free rights to pollute had been stopped from 2013. This means that these industries need to pay a non-supported CO2 price for their emissions. This explain that they adapted faster than other industries.

Other sectors, as aviation, sharply decreased their emissions during the pandemic, but it was mainly because of the economic situation, not because of they adapted their industry. For other polluting sectors as cement and steel, they still mainly supported by generous free rights to pollute these last ten years. The result is quite obvious : they did not reduced their emissions so far.

One of the main argument which explain that these industries still receive free rights to pollute is the risk of "carbon leakage". This means, in other words, that companies might threaten EU authorities if they pay a too high price of CO₂ that they will delocalize their activities to other continents. These companies used this powerful argument for their lobbying so far.

And yet, this is the principle behind the European Union Emissions Trading System : force companies to complain by making them pay a higher price of CO₂. Companies that emit more than their fixed emissions must buy additional permits from those that emit less.

Our analysis also shows that CO₂ prices increased these last years, mainly because authorities decided to reduce the amount of right to pollute under circulation by creating a market stability reserve. This mechanism, created from 2016, seems to be efficient, as the prices became higher since this date.

Even with this tendency, big polluter still pay a lower price for their emissions, due to the free rights to pollute that they received during the last decades. An InfluenceMap recent analysis explains that, according to Bloomberg, steel and cement sectors only pay around 50 euros per ton of CO₂, where the spot price in the market has reached 100 euros per ton.

The path to EU resilience still seems yet long again...

Obstacles Key challenges

  • Complex terminology — deciphering EU ETS–specific vocabulary and abbreviations (country codes, sector classifications) supported by official yet unclear documentation.
  • Mixed data structure — allocations and emissions combined in the same category, requiring separation and reclassification.
  • Data inconsistencies — duplicates, nested subcategories, and industrial sectors appearing in multiple groups.
  • Normalization — replacing cryptic abbreviations (e.g. country codes) with explicit labels for clarity.
